## Week 2: Desert & Lakes

### Day 8: Jodhpur ‚Üí Jaisalmer ‚Äî *September 8, 2027*
**Golden City**

![Jaisalmer Fort](data/pois/images/in/jaisalmer-fort-1.webp)

| | |
|---|---|

- Desert drive, getting remote
- Afternoon: Arrive Jaisalmer - only "living fort" in India

Jaisalmer is India's most remote major city, an outpost on the edge of the Thar Desert just 100km from the Pakistan border. Founded in 1156, the city prospered from caravan trade until the Partition of India in 1947 severed these routes. Jaisalmer Fort is unique among Indian forts - a "living fort" where one-quarter of the old city's population (about 3,000 people) still lives within its 12th-century walls. The entire city appears golden due to the yellow sandstone from which it's built.

Drive west from Jodhpur through increasingly arid landscape - the last section passes through genuine desert. Arrive Jaisalmer mid-afternoon as the low sun makes the sandstone walls glow gold. Accommodation inside the fort itself offers a unique experience of staying within medieval walls. Evening walk through the narrow fort lanes to the ramparts for sunset. Early September temperatures: 34-36¬∞C during the day, cooling to 26¬∞C at night.

Jaisalmer's havelis (mansions) are among India's finest examples of sandstone carving. Built by wealthy merchants in the 18th-19th centuries, these buildings feature incredibly intricate facades with hundreds of carved balconies, screens, and ornamental details. Patwon Ki Haveli, the largest, took 55 years to complete. The Sam Sand Dunes, 42km west of Jaisalmer, offer the classic Thar Desert experience with rolling golden dunes reaching heights of 30-60 meters.

Morning exploring the fort and its Jain temples, then visiting the famous havelis in the lower town - Patwon Ki Haveli and Nathmal Ki Haveli are highlights. Afternoon camel safari departing for the Sam Sand Dunes, timed to arrive for sunset over the dunes. Option to stay overnight at a desert camp or return to the fort hotel. Early September temperatures in the desert: 35-38¬∞C during the day; nights in the desert can drop to 22¬∞C.

Udaipur was founded in 1559 by Maharana Udai Singh II after Akbar conquered the previous Mewar capital of Chittorgarh. Udaipur's City Palace is the largest palace complex in Rajasthan, stretching 244 meters along Lake Pichola.

Long driving day across Rajasthan. Break the journey at Ranakpur for the extraordinary Jain temples - allow at least an hour to appreciate the carved marble. Udaipur's City Palace took 400 years to complete, with successive maharanas adding their own contributions. The palace is now partly a museum and partly the Taj Lake Palace hotel. The octopus scene from the James Bond film Octopussy was filmed at Jag Mandir.

**Salt desert**

- Cross into Gujarat

The Rann of Kutch is the world's largest salt marsh, covering 7,500 square kilometers on the India-Pakistan border. During the monsoon (June-September), the Rann floods; as the water evaporates through October-February, a perfectly flat white salt desert emerges. The Kutch region is home to distinct tribal communities - the Rabari, Ahir, and Jat - known for their embroidery, mirror work, and traditional mud houses. A special permit is required to visit some areas near the border.

Very long driving day west from Udaipur into Gujarat. Cross the Aravalli Hills and descend into the flatlands leading to the Rann. Arrival timed for sunset over the salt flats - the white expanse stretching to the horizon is surreal. Stay at one of the tent resorts on the edge of the Rann. Mid-September: the monsoon water may still partially cover the Rann; by late September the white salt emerges. Temperatures: 30-34¬∞C.

The Kutchi people have developed a distinctive culture adapted to this harsh border region. Villages like Bhuj, Hodka, and Dhordo showcase traditional bhungas (circular mud houses) decorated with intricate mirror work and embroidery that has been passed down through generations. The region's crafts are protected as Geographical Indications - Kutchi embroidery, Rogan art (painting with castor oil), and bandhani tie-dye are world-renowned.

Early morning walk onto the salt flats for sunrise - the white expanse changes color dramatically with the light. Midday visit to a Kutchi village to see traditional crafts being made; Hodka is particularly welcoming to visitors. Late afternoon return to the Rann for sunset. Evening cultural program at the tent resort featuring local music and dance. September temperatures: 30-34¬∞C during the day, pleasant evenings.

Ahmedabad is Gujarat's largest city and was India's first UNESCO World Heritage City (2017), recognized for its remarkable collection of 15th-16th century architecture. The old city's pols (residential neighborhoods) contain wooden houses with exquisite carved facades. Sabarmati Ashram was Mahatma Gandhi's headquarters from 1917-1930 and the starting point of the Salt March. The Adalaj Stepwell (1498) is one of India's finest examples of this uniquely Gujarati architectural form.

Drive east across Gujarat through the industrial belt. Arrive Ahmedabad mid-afternoon. Visit Sabarmati Ashram where Gandhi lived for 12 years - the museum and preserved quarters are moving. Late afternoon at Adalaj Stepwell, five stories deep with intricately carved galleries. Evening walk through the old city pols, particularly Dhal Ni Pol and Mangaldas Ni Pol. Mumbai is India's financial capital and most populous city, with over 20 million people in the metropolitan area. Hampi was the capital of the Vijayanagara Empire from the 14th to 16th centuries, once one of the largest and richest cities in the world with a population of 500,000. The city was destroyed in 1565 after defeat at the Battle of Talikota, leaving behind an extraordinary landscape of ruined temples, palaces, and markets scattered among dramatic granite boulders. The UNESCO World Heritage Site covers 26 square kilometers and contains over 1,600 ruins.

Drive inland from Goa through the Western Ghats into the Karnataka plateau. The landscape changes dramatically as the boulders of Hampi come into view. Arrive mid-afternoon and walk to Virupaksha Temple, the only temple still in active worship. Climb Hemakuta Hill for sunset over the boulder-strewn landscape. Late September temperatures: 28-32¬∞C; monsoon ending, excellent light for photography.

Sanapur Lake, across the river from the main ruins, has become popular for cliff jumping and coracle (round boat) rides among the boulders.

Full day exploring Hampi's ruins. Morning at Vittala Temple - arrive early before tour groups; the stone chariot and musical pillars are unmissable. Midday at the Royal Enclosure and Lotus Mahal. Bangalore (officially Bengaluru) is India's tech capital, home to global companies and a thriving startup scene. The city sits at 900m elevation, giving it a more temperate climate than most of India. Bangalore Palace (1887) was built in Tudor Revival style by the local maharaja after a visit to Windsor Castle. Drive southeast through Karnataka to Bangalore. The highway is good and the landscape transitions from rocky Deccan plateau to more forested terrain around the city. Arrive mid-afternoon. Kochi (Cochin) has been a trading port for over 600 years, attracting Portuguese, Dutch, and British colonizers. Fort Kochi is a peninsula that still contains Portuguese churches, Dutch mansions, and the iconic Chinese fishing nets (cheena vala) introduced by traders from the court of Kublai Khan. The Jewish community established here in the 16th century built what is now India's oldest active synagogue. The spice trade made Kochi wealthy; the famous Mattancherry spice market continues to operate today.

Long driving day descending from Bangalore through the Western Ghats into Kerala. The road is good but winding in places. Arrive Fort Kochi late afternoon. Walk to the waterfront to see the Chinese fishing nets in operation at sunset - the silhouette of these cantilevered structures against the evening sky is iconic. Dinner of Kerala seafood. The Sundarbans is the world's largest mangrove forest, spanning 10,000 square kilometers across the delta where the Ganges, Brahmaputra, and Meghna rivers meet the Bay of Bengal. Shared between Bangladesh and India, it's a UNESCO World Heritage Site and home to approximately 100 Royal Bengal tigers - the only tigers in the world that regularly prey on humans. The forest is named after the sundari trees that dominate the landscape. Local honey collectors (mawalis) enter the forest with legendary rituals and masks worn on the back of their heads to confuse attacking tigers.

Day 54: Early departure from Dhaka, driving southwest to Khulna. Vehicle left in secure parking. Board boat in afternoon and enter the Sundarbans via the Passur River. Evening cruise watching the sunset over endless mangroves. Night on boat with sounds of the forest. Day 55: Full day exploring the channels - spotted deer, saltwater crocodiles, monitor lizards, and over 260 bird species virtually guaranteed. Tiger sightings extremely rare but the atmosphere is unforgettable. Late October: 28-32¬∞C, humidity high but bearable on the water.

Cox's Bazar is home to the world's longest natural unbroken sea beach - 120 kilometers of continuous sandy coastline along the Bay of Bengal.
