### Day 2: Darwin ‚Üí Kakadu ‚Äî *September 28, 2028*
**Australia's largest national park**

| | |
|---|---|

| **Stay** | Camping/Lodge - Kakadu (2 nights) |

- Drive to Kakadu
- Ubirr rock art

Kakadu is Australia's largest national park, a UNESCO World Heritage site for both natural and cultural values. Aboriginal people have lived here continuously for over 65,000 years‚Äîthe rock art at sites like Ubirr and Nourlangie documents thousands of years of human and animal history. The landscape varies from floodplains to sandstone escarpments to monsoon forests.

Morning drive to Kakadu, entering through the northern gate. Afternoon at Ubirr, one of the park's premier rock art sites‚Äîgalleries of paintings spanning thousands of years depict animals, hunting scenes, and creator spirits. Climb to Nadab Lookout for sunset over the vast Nadab floodplain, a quintessential Top End experience. Camping or lodge accommodation in the park.

### Day 3: Kakadu ‚Äî *September 29, 2028*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Stay** | Camping/Lodge - Kakadu |

- Nourlangie rock art
- Yellow Water cruise

Kakadu transforms with the seasons‚Äîthe Dry (May-October) offers accessible roads and concentrated wildlife around waterholes; the Wet (November-April) brings spectacular storms and inaccessible areas. Yellow Water billabong is one of Australia's premier wildlife experiences, with crocodiles, water buffalo, jabiru, and countless other species.

Full day exploring Kakadu's highlights. Morning at Nourlangie for more extraordinary rock art against the backdrop of the Arnhem Land escarpment. Afternoon Yellow Water cruise‚Äîsaltwater crocodiles glide past lotus-filled waters while birds congregate in thousands. If Jim Jim Falls is accessible (check road conditions), the hike to this 200-meter plunge pool is unforgettable.

### Day 4: Kakadu ‚Üí Katherine ‚Äî *September 30, 2028*

|---|---|
| **Distance** | 280 km |
| **Driving** | 3 hrs |
| **Stay** | Hotel/Camping - Katherine |

- Drive south
- Katherine Gorge (Nitmiluk)

Katherine Gorge‚Äîknown by its indigenous name Nitmiluk‚Äîis a series of 13 sandstone gorges carved by the Katherine River over countless millennia. The gorges form a spectacular system best explored by boat, with ancient rock art on the cliff walls and freshwater crocodiles sunning on the banks.

Drive south from Kakadu through savanna woodland to Katherine. Afternoon cruise or kayak through the first two or three gorges‚Äîthe sandstone walls rise 70 meters on either side, their colors shifting in the afternoon light. Rock wallabies perch on impossibly steep ledges. This is Jawoyn country, and cultural tours explain the deep indigenous connection to this place.

### Day 6-7: Kununurra ‚Üí Bungle Bungles ‚Äî *October 2-3, 2028*

- 4WD track to Purnululu

The Bungle Bungle Range in Purnululu National Park was unknown to non-Aboriginal Australians until a TV film crew flew over in 1983. The distinctive beehive-shaped towers, striped orange and black by differential weathering of sandstone layers, create one of Australia's most extraordinary landscapes. The park requires 4WD access.

Two days exploring this unique UNESCO site. The 53 km track from the highway takes 2-3 hours in good conditions‚Äîcheck with park rangers before attempting. Day one: Cathedral Gorge, a natural amphitheater reached by walking through towering domes. Day two: Echidna Chasm, an incredibly narrow slot canyon only lit by the midday sun. Camping under stars in the Australian outback.

### Day 8-10: Bungle Bungles ‚Üí Broome via Gibb River Road ‚Äî *October 4-6, 2028*

| | |
|---|---|

| **Stay** | Stations/Camping along Gibb River Road |

- Australia's legendary 4WD route
- El Questro Station
- Gorges and swimming holes

The Gibb River Road is one of Australia's legendary outback tracks, running 660 km through the heart of the Kimberley from Kununurra to Derby. Originally a cattle road serving remote stations, it now attracts adventurous travelers seeking gorges, waterfalls, and genuine outback experience. The road requires high-clearance 4WD and is typically only open May-October.

Three days of 4WD adventure. Day one: El Questro Wilderness Park, with its gorges and thermal springs. Day two: Manning Gorge, Bell Gorge, and Galvans Gorge‚Äîeach offering pristine swimming holes in spectacular settings. Day three: Complete the Gibb to Derby, then drive to Broome. Carry extra fuel, water, and supplies; this is remote country where self-sufficiency is essential.

### Day 11-12: Broome ‚Äî *October 7-8, 2028*
**Pearls and sunsets**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Stay** | Hotel - Broome (2 nights) |

- Cable Beach sunsets
- Pearl heritage
- Dinosaur footprints
- Rest and resupply

Broome was built on pearling‚Äîfor decades it supplied 80% of the world's mother-of-pearl. Japanese, Chinese, Malay, and Aboriginal divers worked (and often died) harvesting the valuable shells. Today Broome is famous for its spectacular Cable Beach sunsets, when camels walk along the 22 km stretch of white sand against a blazing sky.

Two days in Broome. The obligatory Cable Beach sunset, preferably from camelback. Visit the pearl showrooms and pearling museum to understand the industry that built this town. At very low tide, dinosaur footprints are visible on the rocks at Gantheaume Point‚Äî130 million years old. Resupply for the long coastal drive south.

Ningaloo Reef stretches 260 km along Western Australia's coast‚Äîthe world's largest fringing reef and one of the few places where you can walk directly from shore onto a major reef system. Visit Cape Range National Park's gorges, where red rocks meet turquoise water. If visiting in season (April-July), a whale shark snorkeling tour is a bucket-list experience‚Äîswimming alongside the world's largest fish. Manta rays are possible year-round.

The Nullarbor Plain is one of the world's largest single exposures of limestone, stretching 1,200 km across southern Australia. The Eyre Highway crosses it via what's claimed as the world's longest straight stretch of road (146 km). "Nullarbor" is Latin for "no trees"‚Äîaccurate for much of the crossing.

Three days crossing the great emptiness. Fill up at every fuel stop‚Äîdistances between them can exceed 200 km. The Great Australian Bight provides dramatic coastal scenery, with cliffs dropping sheer to the Southern Ocean. At Head of Bight (June-October), southern right whales calve in the sheltered waters‚Äîone of the world's best land-based whale viewing sites. Arrive Adelaide, gateway to Australia's southeast.

Adelaide is South Australia's capital, a planned city of wide boulevards and parklands. Less frenetic than Sydney or Melbourne, it's known for festivals, wine regions (the Barossa Valley is close), and excellent food. The Central Market is one of Australia's largest undercover markets.

Uluru (Ayers Rock) and Kata Tjuta (the Olgas) are the Red Centre's iconic landmarks‚Äîmassive sandstone formations rising from the flat desert. Uluru is a single rock 348 meters high and 9.4 km around; Kata Tjuta comprises 32 domed rocks with a combined height of 546 meters. Both are sacred to the local Anangu people.

Day 79: Drive from Coober Pedy to Yulara, the resort village serving Uluru. Arrive in time for sunset at the Rock‚Äîwatching Uluru's colors shift through the spectrum is transcendent. Day 80: Sunrise at Uluru (equally spectacular), then base walk around the rock learning about Anangu culture. Afternoon at Kata Tjuta, walking through the Valley of the Winds between the massive domes. This is Australia's spiritual heart.

The Great Ocean Road is Australia's most famous scenic drive, built by returned soldiers after WWI as a memorial. The 243 km route hugs the dramatic Shipwreck Coast, passing through rainforest, surf beaches, and the limestone stacks of the Twelve Apostles.

Full day on the Great Ocean Road, driving east to west for the best ocean views. Stop at the Twelve Apostles‚Äîeight limestone stacks remaining from the original nine (one collapsed in 2005). Walk to London Arch, Loch Ard Gorge, and the Gibson Steps. The Great Otway National Park offers rainforest walks and koala spotting. Arrive Melbourne area by evening.
